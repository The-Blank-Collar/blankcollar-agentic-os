# Vision — The Blank Collar Manifesto

> Work is for bots. Life is for humans.

## The shift

For a hundred years, the same trade has been quietly happening at the centre of the economy:

> A human spends their finite hours producing economic output, and in exchange receives money to buy back a little bit of life.

Most people lose that trade. They give the best decade of their attention to a job that doesn't love them back, and they receive — at best — a comfortable retirement starting at 65.

We don't have to keep making that trade. The cost of producing economic output is collapsing. Agents — software that doesn't sleep, doesn't burn out, doesn't ask for promotions — can now perform a meaningful and growing share of the work that used to require human hours.

**The Blank Collar bet:** the right response to that collapse is not to compete with the bots. It is to *own* them.

## What we are building

A simple, beautiful operating system that lets *one human* run a real business operated almost entirely by agents.

- A solo founder runs a 12-person company.
- A creator runs a media operation with no team.
- A two-person family business has the operational depth of a 50-person agency.

The point of the OS is not to be technically impressive. The point is to make that life feel **normal**.

## The three audiences

| Audience              | What they need from the OS                                            |
|-----------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **The Beginner**      | Press 3 buttons, have a working agent company.                        |
| **The Operator**      | A clean dashboard, no babysitting, weekly review meetings with self.  |
| **The Power User**    | Drill down to raw agents, swap memory backends, build new skills.     |

The OS must serve all three from day one. The first audience is the hardest, and the most important. If a complete beginner can't run it, we've failed — even if the power users love it.

## Why "Blank Collar"?

White-collar implied a desk and a salary. Blue-collar implied tools and shifts. Both implied that the work itself was something a human did with their hands or their mind.

The Blank Collar wears no collar at all. The work is done — but not by them. The collar is blank because the role of "worker" no longer maps to a person.

**It's not a class. It's a state of life.**

You're a Blank Collar the day your company makes more revenue from agents-running-without-you than from hours you personally clocked.

## The product principles

1. **Goals before agents.** The user manages outcomes. The OS manages execution.
2. **Beginner-default, power-user-capable.** Default UX is for someone who has never coded. Every layer below the surface is open for those who want it.
3. **Local-first.** Your company should run on your laptop before it lives on a server.
4. **Persistent brain.** The Company Brain survives every agent, every restart, every model change.
5. **Role-scoped trust.** No single agent gets the keys to the whole company.
6. **Swap, don't lock-in.** Every component is replaceable. We're building an OS, not a moat.
7. **Audit by default.** Every mutation is recorded. Trust comes from transparency.

## The end state

A version of www.blankcollar.ai where someone signs up on a Tuesday afternoon, answers four questions about what they want their company to do, and goes to dinner. By Friday, their agents have set up the storefront, written the first email sequence, scheduled the first social posts, and emailed them a one-page summary asking for two specific decisions.

That person didn't write code. They didn't design an agent. They just said what they wanted.

That is the win condition.

## Why now

Three things have lined up that weren't true 18 months ago:

1. Frontier models are cheap enough to run dozens of agents per user per day.
2. Memory layers (vectors + structured stores + scoped retrieval) are mature enough to build a real Company Brain.
3. The cultural acceptance of "AI doing real work" has flipped — the question is no longer *can* it, but *should* I let it.

The window to define what an "Agentic OS" looks like before the incumbents do is open. We intend to use it.
